## Finance and Accounting Basics

Finance primarily focuses on three questions:

- What resources do I need?
- How do I acquire the funds to obtain those resources?
- How do I efficiently manage those resources once acquired?

There are three key types of players in finance:

- **Entrepreneurs:** Those who need funds.
- **Investors:** Those who have funds to provide.
- **Facilitators:** Institutions that match entrepreneurs with investors (e.g., Banks, Mutual funds, Private equity funds, Insurance companies, Investment banks).

**Accounting** is the system for providing quantitative, primarily financial, information about economic entities, which is useful for making economic decisions.

Types of accounting:

- **Bookkeeping:** The routine gathering and recording of data.
- **Financial Accounting:** Reporting to external stakeholders.
- **Managerial Accounting:** Providing detailed, internal data used to make business decisions.
- **Income Taxes:** Ensuring compliance with tax laws.

## Most Common Financial Reports

The three core financial statements are:

- **Balance Sheet:** A snapshot of a company's assets, liabilities, and equity at a specific point in time.
- **Income Statement:** A report detailing a company's revenues, expenses, and net income over a specific period.
- **Statement of Cash Flows:** A report showing the cash collected and paid by a company during a specific period.

### Balance Sheet

The balance sheet is built on the accounting equation:

```mermaid

    A[Assets] --> EQ[Liabilities + Equity]

```

- **Assets:** Resources owned or controlled by a company providing probable future benefit.
- **Liabilities:** Financial obligations or debts owed to another party.
- **Equity:** Direct investment plus retained profits.

### Income Statement

The fundamental equation for the income statement is:

```mermaid
graph LR
    R[Revenues] --> E[Expenses]
    E --> NI[Net Income]
```

- **Revenue:** The value of assets generated from selling goods or services.

### The Statement Of Cash Flows

This statement tracks three main types of cash flow activities:

1. **Operating Activities:** Outflows like wages, utilities, taxes, and interest.
2. **Investing Activities:** Outflows for new buildings, land, or machinery.
3. **Financing Activities:** Inflows from borrowing money or securing new investments from owners.

## Techniques for Using Financial Reports

**Financial ratio analysis** examines the relationships among numbers on financial statements. It is used to compare a single company over time or to compare multiple companies simultaneously.

### Return on Equity (ROE)

Calculated by dividing net income by equity, ROE measures the profit earned per dollar of owner investment, based on historical data.

**Market return** is a combination of dividends received and the increase in the share's market value.

### The DuPont Framework

The DuPont framework breaks ROE into three components:

- **Profitability:** Efficiency in controlling expenses to maximize profit from sales.
- **Efficiency:** Effectiveness in utilizing assets to generate sales.
- **Leverage:** The extent to which borrowed money amplifies the initial equity investment.

## Short-Term Financial Management

The **operating cycle** is the time it takes to buy inventory, sell it, and collect the cash.

A lengthy operating cycle ties up cash. Cash shortfalls are inconvenient and costly, so businesses must balance keeping sufficient cash without holding too much. Cash budgets are essential tools for this. Experience and strong supplier relationships can also help manage cash flow (e.g., strategically delaying payments). Another option is securing a short-term bank loan.

![Operating Cycle](/assets/images/finance-for-non-financial-managers/operating-cycle.png)

Selling on credit is a proven marketing strategy to attract customers and boost sales.

![Selling on Credit](/assets/images/finance-for-non-financial-managers/selling-on-credit.png)

**The Goldilocks Principle:** Companies aim to minimize the money tied up in receivables and inventory while maintaining enough to ensure smooth operations.

## Costing a Product or Service

Understanding costs is crucial for pricing and profitability:

- **Variable Costs:** Change based on production volume (e.g., materials).
- **Fixed Costs:** Remain constant regardless of production (e.g., rent, salaries).
- **Contribution Margin:** The difference between the selling price and variable costs.
- **Overhead Costs:** Business expenses not directly tied to revenue generation.

**Break-Even Point:**
The point where total revenues exactly cover total costs (no profit, no loss). It is vital to calculate this _before_ launching a business or product. To plan for a specific profit level (Target Profit), it can be treated as an additional fixed cost in calculations.

## Creating a Budget

**Responsibility Accounting:** Individuals are held accountable only for the financial inflows and outflows they can control.

![Budgeting](/assets/images/finance-for-non-financial-managers/budgeting.png)

A budget is necessary to analyze the variances between planned expectations and actual results, enabling better future decision-making.

## Income Tax Basics

While income taxes are complex, the fundamental concepts include:

- **Tax Brackets:** You pay a higher tax rate only on the portion of your income that falls into a higher bracket.
- **Tax Deductions and Credits:** Deductions reduce your taxable income (e.g., retirement plan contributions, mortgage interest), lowering your overall tax burden.
